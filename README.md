---

## Research Question

To what extent are HR occupations exposed to Generative AI automation, and which specific task clusters and skills drive that exposure?

---

## Key Findings

- **HR Assistants** are most at risk (AI Exposure Score: 3.95/5.0) — 58% of their tasks are highly automatable
- **Education Administrators** are most resilient (Score: 3.00/5.0) — only 22% high-risk tasks
- **Active Listening and Speaking** (importance score: 4.08) are simultaneously the most critical and most AI-resistant HR skills
- **38% of all HR tasks** analyzed are highly automatable by Gen AI
- Even senior roles such as **HR Managers score in the medium-risk range** (3.31/5.0) — no HR role is untouched

## Methodology

1. Downloaded O*NET 30.2 task and skills data for 8 HR occupations
2. Filtered 18,796 tasks to 180 HR-specific tasks across 8 SOC codes
3. Scored each task 1-5 for Gen AI exposure using keyword-based rubric grounded in McKinsey and WEF automation frameworks
4. Calculated average exposure score per occupation
5. Cross-referenced with O*NET skills importance ratings to identify AI-resistant competencies

---

## HR Recommendations

1. **Reskill HR Assistants Urgently** — with 58% of their tasks automatable, invest in data literacy and AI tool management programs
2. **Redesign HR Roles Around AI Augmentation** — pair human judgment with AI efficiency rather than treating Gen AI as a replacement threat
3. **Invest in Human Skills Development** — Active Listening, Social Perceptiveness, and Judgment and Decision Making are the most AI-resistant HR competencies
4. **Conduct an Internal HR Task Audit** — map your department's task portfolio against AI exposure frameworks using O*NET SOC codes
5. **Build Organization-Wide AI Literacy** — every HR function, including senior roles, needs foundational AI literacy to lead effectively in an AI-augmented workplace
